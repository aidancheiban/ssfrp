Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory

Research Projects

Each summer, student teams work on focused research projects in the field and lab, mentored by Columbia University and LDEO scientists.

2025

This Summer's Projects

Image Placeholder

Drones

Naomi & Eva

Using a thermal drone to map temperature gradients and elevation across Piermont Marsh, this team is tracking vegetation and water level changes from 2024 to 2025. They link aerial hot and cold spots to field samples to build a clearer picture of marsh health.

Image Placeholder

Marsh Microplastics

Tahsin & Sidney

This team investigates where different types of microplastics accumulate in Piermont Marsh. Students extract sediment cores by canoe, then analyze samples in the lab using X-rays and microscopes to understand how microplastic distribution puts ecosystems at risk.

Image Placeholder

Carbon

Tangie & Tommy

The carbon team measures methane and CO2 concentrations at sampling sites in and around Piermont Marsh. Using tools like XRF spectroscopy, Picarro, Loss of Ignition, and Gas Chromatography, they work to understand how carbon moves through wetland ecosystems.

Image Placeholder

Redox

Sara & Grace

Students collect sediment cores from tidal pools and extract pore water to study how redox reactions in the marsh soil control the solubility of toxic metals like uranium and arsenic. Lab work includes ICP-MS and X-ray diffraction and fluorescence analysis.

Image Placeholder

Water Treatment

Caden & James

Each student designs their own water filter using CAD software and 3D printing, then tests it against water contaminated with arsenic. The project covers redox chemistry, filtration science, and hands-on engineering to tackle a real public health problem.

Image Placeholder

Air Pollution

Haowen & Angus

Working across New York City, this team uses UPAS and PurpleAir sensors to measure PM2.5 levels at various outdoor and indoor sites. Students also take monitors home to measure kitchen air quality, connecting their data to asthma exposure research.

Image Placeholder

Sand Microplastics

Jen & YongHong

This lab-focused team extracts microplastics from raw sand samples collected in the remote desert of Oman, using a Density Separation Device and Raman Spectroscope. Their goal is to understand how microplastics spread in areas with minimal human activity.

Image Placeholder

Forest Soil

Niamh, Zoe, Aidan C. & Antoun

Students collect soil samples across Lamont Sanctuary Forest and test them for carbon, nitrogen, pH, and aggregate stability. Data is visualized using mapping software to explore how soil properties vary spatially and what drives those patterns.

Image Placeholder

Forest Spectroscopy

Niamh, Zoe, Aidan C. & Antoun

Using a spectrometer to measure how soil reflects light at different wavelengths, this team compares their readings with satellite imagery to build models that predict soil properties across the forest and calculate how much carbon it can store.

Image Placeholder

Temperature

Aidan S. & Benjamin

In collaboration with the Drones team, this group uses thermal drones, FLIR cameras, and soil probes to compare heat distribution in Piermont Marsh and Lamont Forest. Python is used for data visualization and to identify areas of vegetation stress.

Last Summer's Projects

Image Placeholder

Microplastics — Laundry

Jennifer & Julie

This team investigated microfiber emissions from laundry dryers at commercial laundromats and Columbia University. They categorized fibers by size and studied which types enter the human body through air pathways.

Image Placeholder

Microplastics — Marsh

Kemra & Sidney

Students focused on the decomposition rate of microplastics in Piermont Marsh sediment. Core samples were analyzed using trace metal dating and density separation to link microplastic concentrations at specific depths to US plastic production history.

Image Placeholder

Forest

Aidan C., Antoun, Niamh & Zoe

Working near the Lamont Comer building, this team sampled soil for organic carbon, nitrogen, and moisture. Field spectrometers and drone imagery were used to map how these properties vary and how climate change affects forest and soil health.

Image Placeholder

Drones

Jaden & Chanry

Using photogrammetry, this team built high-resolution 3D elevation models of Piermont Marsh and the Lamont Forest. Multispectral imaging was used to assess vegetation health and coverage, creating a baseline dataset for future monitoring.

Image Placeholder

Redox

Sara & Grace

Students collected and analyzed sediment cores from an isolated marsh pond to map chemical redox zones with depth. ICP-MS analysis of extracted pore water revealed how redox reactions drive metal concentrations in the sediment.

Image Placeholder

Carbon

Tangie & Aidan S.

This team measured methane flux and carbon dynamics at multiple sites in Piermont Marsh, collecting peat and pore water samples. They also studied how marsh vegetation affects the wetland's capacity to store carbon and methane.

Image Placeholder

Air Pollution

Ninh & Saya

Using PurpleAir sensors, this team measured PM2.5 levels at some of New York City's busiest subway stations. They compared how climate conditions, station age, and infrastructure affect air quality and the health of daily commuters and MTA workers.

Image Placeholder

Water Treatment

Vivian & Tenzin

Focused on arsenic contamination in private wells used by Native American communities, this team built water filters using iron-oxidizing bacteria from marsh water. They tested filter performance by measuring element concentrations before and after filtration at Lamont.
